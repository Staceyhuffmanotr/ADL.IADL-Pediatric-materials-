ADL and IADL in Pediatric Occupational Therapy

Ages 5-18: Development, Assessment, and Intervention

Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, students will be able to:

  • Assess and analyze age-appropriate ADL and IADL performance in children ages 5-18
  • Demonstrate knowledge of typical development patterns for daily living skills
  • Understand the impact of various conditions on occupational performance
  • Apply evidence-based evaluation methods and intervention strategies
  • Consider cultural, environmental, and contextual factors influencing participation

Key Principles for Pediatric ADL/IADL Intervention

Child-Centered Approach

  • Natural Contexts: Practice in real environments where skills will be used
  • Family Collaboration: Include caregivers in goal setting and intervention
  • Cultural Sensitivity: Respect family values, routines, and expectations
  • Developmental Readiness: Match interventions to child's cognitive and physical abilities

Evidence-Based Training Techniques

Technique Description Best Used For
Forward Chaining Child completes first step, practitioner completes rest; gradually add steps Multi-step tasks like morning routines
Backward Chaining Practitioner completes most steps, child completes final step; work backward Tasks where completion is reinforcing (tooth brushing)
Task Grading Modify difficulty level of activity components Adapting for motor or cognitive challenges
Environmental Modification Change physical or social environment to support success Sensory sensitivities, physical limitations
Visual Supports Pictures, schedules, checklists to support memory and sequencing Executive function challenges, autism

Cultural and Contextual Considerations

Family-Centered Practice

  • Respect Cultural Values: Understand family beliefs about independence, gender roles, and daily routines
  • Language Considerations: Provide materials in family's preferred language
  • Economic Factors: Consider financial constraints when recommending equipment or modifications
  • Living Situation: Adapt goals to apartment vs. house, shared vs. private spaces
  • Family Structure: Account for single parents, extended family, multiple caregivers

Questions to Ask Families

  1. What daily activities are most important to your family?
  2. Who typically helps with these activities?
  3. What time of day works best for practice?
  4. Are there cultural or religious considerations?
  5. What has worked or not worked in the past?
  6. What are your expectations for independence?
