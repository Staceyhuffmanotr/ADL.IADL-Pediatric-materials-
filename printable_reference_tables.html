Table 3: Intervention Techniques for ADL/IADL Training
Technique Description Best Used For Example
Forward Chaining Child completes first step independently, practitioner completes remaining steps. Gradually add steps from beginning. Complex multi-step tasks, children who need success early in sequence Morning routine: Child wakes up, gets out of bed. Practitioner does rest, then child does wake up + brush teeth, etc.
Backward Chaining Practitioner completes most steps, child completes final step successfully. Work backward through sequence. Tasks where completion is reinforcing, children who benefit from immediate success Tooth brushing: Practitioner does brushing, child spits and rinses. Then child brushes + spits + rinses, etc.
Task Grading Systematically modify difficulty level of activity components (motor, cognitive, sensory demands). Adapting for various ability levels, progressive skill building Dressing: Start with loose clothing, then fitted, then fasteners, then complex coordination
Modeling Practitioner demonstrates actions; shows child how to complete action or uses video demonstration. Visual learners, children with autism, new skill acquisition Hair combing: Show proper grip, direction of combing, sectioning hair
Partial Participation Child engages in those aspects of occupation for which they are able, others provide assistance for remaining components. Significant disabilities, building engagement and choice-making Dressing: Child chooses clothes and holds arm out, caregiver does fastening
Environmental Modification Change physical or social environment to support successful performance. Sensory sensitivities, physical limitations, attention challenges Bathroom: Add grab bars, non-slip mat, better lighting, reduce visual clutter
Visual Supports Use pictures, schedules, checklists, or written cues to support memory and sequencing. Executive function challenges, autism, memory difficulties Morning routine chart with pictures of each step in sequence
Table 4: Condition-Specific Adaptations for ADL/IADL
Condition Common Challenges Intervention Strategies
Autism Spectrum Disorder
  • Sensory sensitivities
  • Routine preferences
  • Communication challenges
  • Transitions difficulties
  • Visual schedules and social stories
  • Sensory adaptations (different textures, lighting)
  • Graduated exposure to new experiences
  • Consistent routines and preparation for changes
  • Choice-making opportunities within structure
ADHD
  • Attention difficulties
  • Impulsivity
  • Organization challenges
  • Time management
  • Break tasks into smaller steps
  • Use timers and visual cues
  • Create organized, clutter-free environment
  • Build in movement breaks
  • Use checklists and reminders
Cerebral Palsy
  • Motor limitations
  • Coordination difficulties
  • Positioning needs
  • Fatigue
  • Adaptive equipment and positioning aids
  • Energy conservation techniques
  • Motor learning approaches
  • Environmental modifications for accessibility
  • Compensatory strategies
Intellectual Disability
  • Processing speed
  • Memory challenges
  • Generalization difficulties
  • Abstract thinking
  • Repetitive practice in natural environments
  • Consistent routines and expectations
  • Concrete, step-by-step instructions
  • Family training and support
  • Positive reinforcement systems
Sensory Processing Disorders
  • Over/under responsivity
  • Sensory seeking/avoiding
  • Regulation difficulties
  • Motor planning challenges
  • Sensory diet and regulation strategies
  • Environmental modifications for sensory needs
  • Gradual desensitization programs
  • Sensory tools and adaptive equipment
  • Alternative textures and materials
Visual Impairments
  • Spatial orientation
  • Object identification
  • Safety awareness
  • Organization
  • Tactile and auditory cues
  • Consistent organization systems
  • High contrast materials
  • Magnification and lighting
  • Mobility and orientation training
